# Domain Context: Content Creation

## Scope
Content creation covers the planning, production, governance, distribution, optimization, and maintenance of content across editorial, educational, marketing, social, product, and internal communication contexts. It includes both human-authored and AI-assisted workflows.

## Core Principles
- Audience first: content is designed for a specific reader, viewer, listener, or participant with identifiable goals, constraints, and prior knowledge.
- Purpose before format: the job the content must do comes before channel or medium selection.
- Clarity over cleverness: comprehension, actionability, and relevance matter more than stylistic flourish.
- Accuracy and trust: factual claims, numbers, quotes, and attributions must be verified before publication.
- Usefulness over output volume: more content is not better unless it solves a real user need.
- Consistency: tone, terminology, claims, and visual identity must align across channels and campaigns.
- Accessibility by default: content should be usable by people with different abilities, devices, bandwidth, and literacy levels.
- Findability matters: content has limited value if intended users cannot discover it when needed.
- Iterative improvement: publishing is a checkpoint, not the end of the process.
- Ethical communication: content should inform and persuade without manipulation, deception, or avoidable harm.

## Primary Goals
- Inform: explain something accurately and efficiently.
- Persuade: influence attitudes or decisions through credible argument and framing.
- Enable action: help users complete a task, make a decision, or solve a problem.
- Educate: support learning, retention, transfer, and reflection.
- Build trust: reinforce credibility, transparency, and brand reliability.
- Retain attention: sustain engagement long enough for the intended outcome.
- Convert: generate a measurable downstream action such as signup, purchase, inquiry, or adoption.
- Support relationships: nurture communities, customers, stakeholders, employees, or learners over time.

## Content Types
- Editorial: articles, features, op-eds, explainers, news updates.
- Marketing: landing pages, campaign copy, ads, newsletters, case studies.
- Educational: lessons, tutorials, guides, assessments, learning objects.
- Product: onboarding flows, help center articles, release notes, UI copy.
- Social: short-form posts, threads, community updates, creator collaborations.
- Audio/video: podcasts, scripts, webinars, short-form video, long-form video.
- Internal: policies, memos, onboarding docs, executive communications.
- Thought leadership: white papers, research summaries, keynote narratives, industry commentary.
- Community content: discussion prompts, moderation guidance, user-generated content frameworks.

## Audience Model
- Primary audience: the main group the content is intended to serve.
- Secondary audience: adjacent readers who may discover or rely on the content.
- User intent: what the audience is trying to know, feel, or do.
- Prior knowledge: assumptions about expertise, vocabulary, and context.
- Motivation and friction: why the audience cares and what might block comprehension or action.
- Context of use: device, platform, timing, setting, and emotional state.
- Accessibility needs: visual, auditory, cognitive, linguistic, and motor considerations.
- Cultural context: norms, references, examples, humor, and sensitivities vary by audience.

## Quality Standards
- Editorial review for any public-facing or high-impact content.
- Style guide adherence: defined voice, tone, formatting, terminology, capitalization, and punctuation standards.
- Accessibility compliance: headings, meaningful link text, alt text, captions, transcripts, contrast, and readable structure.
- Source verification: claims must be backed by reliable sources appropriate to the topic and risk level.
- Structural quality: strong information hierarchy, scannability, and coherent narrative flow.
- SEO or discoverability alignment where relevant: title, metadata, schema, internal links, keyword coverage, and search intent match.
- Channel fit: the content must respect the norms, limits, and interaction patterns of the distribution channel.
- Legal and policy review for regulated, comparative, health, financial, employment, or privacy-sensitive content.
- Content brief required before production begins for substantial assets or campaigns.
- Defined owner, approver, and review date for evergreen or operational content.
- Measurement plan established before publication when performance matters.

## Editorial and Communication Principles
- Lead with the most important information.
- Match depth to audience need: enough context to understand, not so much that signal is buried.
- Prefer concrete nouns, active voice, and precise verbs.
- Reduce cognitive load through chunking, headings, examples, and progressive disclosure.
- Define unfamiliar terms when first introduced.
- Use examples, scenarios, and demonstrations to bridge abstraction and application.
- Distinguish fact, interpretation, recommendation, and opinion clearly.
- Show evidence proportionate to the claim.
- Avoid ambiguity in calls to action, deadlines, eligibility, and next steps.
- Eliminate filler, redundancy, and decorative jargon.

## Workflow Stages
- Discovery: identify audience, purpose, constraints, stakeholders, and success criteria.
- Research: gather sources, interview subject-matter experts, audit competitors or prior materials, and clarify risks.
- Strategy: decide message architecture, channel mix, format, and distribution approach.
- Briefing: document audience, objective, key points, constraints, references, and deliverables.
- Creation: draft copy, script, visuals, layouts, metadata, and supporting assets.
- Review: editorial, factual, brand, accessibility, legal, and stakeholder review.
- Production: formatting, publishing preparation, QA, tagging, asset optimization, scheduling.
- Distribution: publish, promote, syndicate, and adapt per channel.
- Measurement: monitor reach, engagement, completion, conversion, satisfaction, and qualitative feedback.
- Maintenance: update, consolidate, redirect, archive, or retire content.

## Content Strategy Components
- Content model: define reusable content types, fields, and relationships.
- Taxonomy and metadata: categories, tags, authorship, dates, status, audience, funnel stage.
- Governance: who creates, reviews, approves, publishes, updates, and retires content.
- Editorial calendar: a planning mechanism for cadence, campaigns, launches, and dependencies.
- Lifecycle management: draft, review, approved, published, updated, archived.
- Distribution strategy: owned, earned, paid, and shared channels.
- Repurposing plan: adapt core ideas into multiple formats without fragmenting message integrity.
- Content operations: tools, templates, workflows, SLAs, and responsibilities.

## Channel-Specific Considerations
- Web articles: scannability, headings, internal linking, search intent, and fast time-to-value.
- Email: subject line clarity, preview text, CTA focus, segmentation, and unsubscribe compliance.
- Social media: brevity, hook strength, platform conventions, timing, and community interaction.
- Video: script pacing, visual reinforcement, captions, retention hooks, and thumbnail/title fit.
- Audio: spoken clarity, pacing, transitions, and show note support.
- Product/UI content: brevity, consistency, action orientation, and contextual relevance.
- Documentation: completeness, accuracy, task orientation, version specificity, and update discipline.
- Learning content: sequencing, examples, practice, feedback, and assessment alignment.

## Accessibility and Inclusion
- Use headings and lists to support navigation and screen readers.
- Write descriptive link text rather than vague prompts like “click here”.
- Provide alt text that serves the user’s task, not decorative narration.
- Caption video and provide transcripts for audio/video whenever possible.
- Avoid relying only on color, position, or sound to convey meaning.
- Aim for plain language where feasible without losing necessary precision.
- Be careful with idioms, cultural references, humor, and metaphors that may not translate.
- Represent people, scenarios, and examples inclusively and respectfully.
- Consider readability, localization, and multilingual adaptation early rather than as an afterthought.

## SEO and Discoverability
- Start from audience intent, not just keywords.
- Align titles, headings, metadata, and body content with the questions users actually ask.
- Use internal links to connect related content and reduce orphan pages.
- Maintain canonical content to avoid duplication and search dilution.
- Optimize structured data, snippets, and metadata where relevant.
- Refresh evergreen content to preserve rankings and trust.
- Search performance should not override accuracy, readability, or user value.

## Quality Assurance Checklist
- Goal and audience are explicit.
- Claims are sourced and dates are current.
- Terminology is consistent with the style guide and domain vocabulary.
- Headline, intro, and CTA are aligned.
- Accessibility requirements are met.
- Grammar, spelling, and formatting are clean.
- Links work and point to the intended destinations.
- Data, quotes, names, and citations are verified.
- Legal and compliance checks are completed where needed.
- Analytics tags, metadata, and distribution settings are in place.

## Common Risks
- Audience mismatch: wrong reading level, assumed knowledge, or irrelevant framing.
- Factual errors: unverified claims, outdated data, invented sources, or misquoted experts.
- Brand inconsistency: tone, terminology, or visual style conflicts across channels.
- Strategic drift: content gets produced because a format exists, not because a need exists.
- Copyright and attribution failures: plagiarism, missing permissions, or unlicensed media.
- Legal exposure: unsupported claims, defamation, regulatory violations, or missing disclosures.
- Accessibility failure: content technically published but unusable for part of the audience.
- SEO distortion: keyword stuffing, thin content, duplicated pages, or clickbait titles.
- Staleness: content becomes outdated without ownership or review schedules.
- Distribution mismatch: strong content underperforms because channel fit is poor.
- Measurement failure: success cannot be assessed because goals and metrics were undefined.
- AI-related errors: hallucinations, fabricated citations, style drift, hidden bias, privacy leakage, or generic outputs.
- Overproduction: teams create too much low-value content and starve maintenance.
- Fragmentation: multiple overlapping pages compete, confuse, or contradict one another.

## AI-Assisted Content Creation
- AI is best treated as an accelerator for ideation, outlining, drafting variants, summarization, transformation, and adaptation.
- Human review remains necessary for accuracy, nuance, ethics, legal exposure, and audience fit.
- Prompting should specify audience, objective, constraints, format, tone, and sources.
- Source-grounded generation is safer than free-form generation for factual work.
- Editorial standards should apply equally to human-written and AI-assisted content.
- Teams should define when disclosure is required for AI assistance.
- Sensitive, proprietary, or personal data should not be exposed to external tools without approval.
- AI outputs should be checked for sameness, flattening of voice, and unearned certainty.
- AI can improve speed, but without governance it can multiply low-quality or inconsistent content.

## Review and Approval Model
- Author: responsible for the first draft and intent alignment.
- Editor: improves structure, clarity, coherence, and adherence to style.
- Subject-matter expert: validates factual and domain-specific accuracy.
- Designer or producer: ensures visual, multimedia, and platform execution quality.
- Legal/compliance reviewer: assesses regulated claims, privacy, and disclosure needs when relevant.
- Publisher or channel owner: confirms metadata, scheduling, and distribution readiness.
- Final approver: accountable for release of high-risk or high-visibility content.

## Best Practices
- Create a clear content brief before drafting.
- Use message architecture: define the main point, supporting points, evidence, and CTA.
- Build modular content blocks that can be reused across channels.
- Maintain a content calendar with themes, owners, dependencies, and review dates.
- Establish a structured review sequence rather than ad hoc feedback loops.
- Version evergreen content and log substantive changes.
- Start with audience questions and objections, not internal talking points.
- Define performance metrics before publishing.
- Use templates for recurring formats to reduce variance and speed production.
- Create a single source of truth for approved claims, facts, bios, and brand language.
- Repurpose intentionally: adapt the core idea to each channel rather than copy-pasting.
- Archive or consolidate redundant content regularly.
- Pair quantitative metrics with qualitative feedback to avoid shallow optimization.
- Test headlines, thumbnails, hooks, or CTAs where the channel supports experimentation.
- Document lessons learned from high- and low-performing content.

## Metrics and Evaluation
- Reach: impressions, views, opens, unique visitors.
- Engagement: time on page, scroll depth, watch time, saves, shares, comments, replies.
- Consumption quality: completion rate, bounce rate, dwell time, return visits.
- Conversion: click-through rate, signup rate, lead generation, purchase, activation.
- Retention and loyalty: repeat readership, subscriber retention, community participation.
- Search performance: rankings, click-through from search, non-branded traffic.
- Content efficiency: production time, cost, reuse rate, time to publish.
- Quality signals: error rate, correction rate, user satisfaction, support deflection, task success.
- Learning outcomes where relevant: comprehension, recall, assessment performance, behavior change.

## Governance and Operating Model
- Every important content asset should have an owner.
- Ownership includes maintenance, not just initial publication.
- High-risk domains need stricter approval and evidence standards.
- Taxonomy, templates, naming conventions, and storage rules reduce operational chaos.
- Governance should balance control with creator autonomy.
- Documentation of workflows, roles, and standards is part of quality management.
- Sunset criteria should be explicit for outdated, duplicated, or low-value content.

## Maintenance and Lifecycle
- Evergreen content requires scheduled review.
- Time-sensitive content needs clear timestamps and expiry logic.
- Update rather than duplicate when the topic remains fundamentally the same.
- Archive outdated content that may confuse users if left live.
- Redirect retired URLs where discoverability and SEO matter.
- Track content debt: broken links, stale screenshots, obsolete claims, inconsistent terminology.
- Maintenance capacity should be planned alongside production capacity.

## Common Heuristics
- Can the intended audience understand the first screen or paragraph quickly?
- Is the main claim supported?
- Does each section earn its place?
- Is the next action obvious?
- Would the content still make sense out of platform context?
- Is it credible enough to be quoted or shared publicly?
- Could any statement be misread as stronger, broader, or more certain than intended?

## Prior Art
- Inverted pyramid for news and announcement writing.
- AIDA for persuasive and marketing-oriented messaging.
- PAS (Problem, Agitation, Solution) for conversion-focused copy.
- Plain Language guidelines for public-sector and enterprise communication.
- Chicago Manual of Style, AP Stylebook, and in-house editorial style guides.
- Content design and UX writing practices from digital product teams.
- Topic clusters and pillar-page models for search-oriented content strategy.
- Learning design frameworks for educational content.
- Jobs-to-be-Done framing for audience need discovery.
- Story structure frameworks for narrative and campaign content.

## Domain Vocabulary
- Content brief: planning document defining goals, audience, constraints, and deliverables.
- Editorial calendar: schedule of planned content production and publication.
- Voice: stable brand personality expressed through language.
- Tone: situational variation in how voice is expressed.
- CTA: the action the audience is expected to take next.
- Evergreen content: durable content that remains useful over time.
- Repurposing: adapting a core asset into other formats or channels.
- Metadata: descriptive fields that support organization, distribution, and discovery.
- Content operations: the people, process, and tooling system behind content production.
- Content debt: accumulated maintenance burden from outdated, duplicated, or poor-quality content.
