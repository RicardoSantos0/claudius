# Domain Context: Research

## Scope
Research is the systematic production of knowledge through clearly defined questions, appropriate methods, disciplined evidence collection, critical interpretation, and transparent reporting. It spans basic, applied, evaluative, exploratory, descriptive, explanatory, and design-oriented inquiry across quantitative, qualitative, mixed-methods, computational, and theoretical traditions.

## Core Principles
- Rigor: conclusions must follow from evidence, method, and logic rather than preference or convenience.
- Transparency: research decisions, procedures, materials, assumptions, and limitations must be documented clearly enough to be understood and scrutinized.
- Objectivity: researchers should actively identify bias, alternative explanations, and contradictory evidence.
- Replicability and reproducibility: methods and analyses should be detailed enough that others can repeat the work or reproduce the findings.
- Falsifiability: strong research claims should be framed so that they could, in principle, be disproven.
- Proportionality of claims: conclusions should not exceed what the design, evidence, and context justify.
- Ethical responsibility: research must respect participants, communities, institutions, and the broader consequences of knowledge production.
- Cumulative knowledge: studies should engage with prior work and position new findings within an existing body of evidence.
- Uncertainty awareness: uncertainty is part of research and should be quantified, described, and communicated rather than hidden.
- Intellectual honesty: null results, ambiguities, and failures are part of the evidence base.

## Primary Goals
- Describe phenomena accurately.
- Explain relationships, mechanisms, and causes where appropriate.
- Predict outcomes or behaviors under specified conditions.
- Evaluate interventions, programs, systems, or policies.
- Develop or refine theories, models, constructs, or frameworks.
- Generate hypotheses for future study.
- Support evidence-informed decisions without overstating certainty.
- Contribute durable knowledge that others can assess, build on, or challenge.

## Research Types
- Basic research: seeks conceptual or theoretical understanding without immediate practical application.
- Applied research: addresses practical problems or decisions in real-world settings.
- Exploratory research: investigates poorly understood topics to identify patterns, concepts, or hypotheses.
- Confirmatory research: tests prespecified hypotheses or predictions.
- Descriptive research: characterizes populations, behaviors, environments, or trends.
- Explanatory research: investigates why or how something happens.
- Evaluative research: assesses effectiveness, impact, implementation, or value.
- Design research: develops and studies artifacts, interventions, or processes iteratively.
- Longitudinal research: examines change over time.
- Cross-sectional research: examines a phenomenon at one point or period in time.
- Comparative research: contrasts groups, cases, systems, or conditions.
- Translational research: moves findings toward practice, implementation, or policy.

## Research Questions
- Research questions should be specific, answerable, and aligned with the chosen method.
- Good questions clarify the population, phenomenon, outcome, context, and timescale.
- Questions should distinguish whether the goal is description, explanation, prediction, or evaluation.
- Confirmatory work should specify directional or non-directional hypotheses where appropriate.
- Exploratory work should be framed openly without disguising post hoc discovery as prior expectation.
- Questions should be feasible given available data, expertise, ethics, and time.

## Quality Standards
- Research questions must be precise, coherent, and appropriately scoped.
- A methodology section should be complete before data collection begins for confirmatory studies.
- Sampling strategy, inclusion criteria, and exclusion criteria should be explicit.
- Sample size and statistical power should be justified where relevant.
- Measures, instruments, and operational definitions should be documented clearly.
- Analysis plans should match the data structure, design, and research question.
- Contradictory findings must be reported rather than suppressed.
- Limitations, assumptions, and threats to validity must be acknowledged.
- Data provenance and preprocessing steps must be transparent.
- References should accurately represent prior literature rather than selectively supporting a preferred conclusion.
- Claims should be supported by evidence of appropriate quality and scope.
- High-stakes findings should receive additional review before informing major decisions.

## Research Design Principles
- Align design with the question: descriptive questions do not justify causal claims.
- Choose units of analysis carefully: individual, group, organization, document, event, or system.
- Define constructs before measurement.
- Distinguish between observational and experimental evidence.
- Build comparison into the design where feasible.
- Anticipate confounders, alternative explanations, and contextual influences.
- Separate measurement decisions from desired outcomes.
- Plan for data quality, missingness, attrition, and protocol deviations.
- Treat analysis as part of design, not as something improvised after collection.
- Protect interpretability by keeping the link between question, design, data, and inference explicit.

## Methodological Traditions

### Quantitative Research
- Uses numerical measurement, structured designs, and statistical analysis.
- Common goals include estimation, association testing, modeling, prediction, and causal inference.
- Requires attention to measurement reliability, validity, assumptions, and uncertainty.
- Common designs include experiments, quasi-experiments, surveys, observational studies, and secondary data analysis.

### Qualitative Research
- Investigates meaning, experience, process, context, discourse, and social interaction.
- Common methods include interviews, focus groups, observation, ethnography, case studies, document analysis, and thematic analysis.
- Quality depends on depth, reflexivity, transparency, contextual sensitivity, and interpretive credibility.
- Transferability often matters more than broad statistical generalization.

### Mixed Methods
- Combines qualitative and quantitative approaches to answer complex questions.
- Can be sequential or concurrent.
- Useful when one method complements the weaknesses of another.
- Requires explicit integration rather than parallel but disconnected analyses.

### Computational and Data-Driven Research
- Uses digital trace data, simulations, machine learning, text mining, network analysis, or other computational methods.
- Requires careful attention to feature construction, data-generating processes, model validity, fairness, and reproducibility.
- Predictive performance does not by itself establish explanation or causation.

## Evidence and Inference
- Correlation does not imply causation.
- Statistical significance does not equal practical importance.
- Absence of evidence is not always evidence of absence.
- Evidence quality depends on design, measurement, execution, analysis, and context.
- Triangulation strengthens credibility when multiple methods converge.
- Single studies should rarely be treated as definitive.
- Effect sizes, confidence intervals, uncertainty estimates, and robustness checks often matter more than binary significance thresholds.
- Causal claims require design or reasoning that addresses counterfactuals and confounding.
- External validity depends on similarity of context, population, and conditions.

## Validity Framework
- Construct validity: are the concepts being measured appropriately?
- Internal validity: can the study credibly support its claimed inferences within the study setting?
- External validity: do the findings generalize beyond the immediate sample or context?
- Statistical conclusion validity: are the analytical procedures adequate for the claims made?
- Ecological validity: does the study reflect realistic conditions of the phenomenon?
- Qualitative credibility: are interpretations grounded, coherent, and well supported by evidence?
- Dependability and confirmability: are the procedures traceable and interpretations accountable?

## Sampling and Recruitment
- Sampling should follow the inferential goal, not convenience alone.
- Define the target population explicitly.
- Use appropriate recruitment procedures consistent with ethics and feasibility.
- Document inclusion and exclusion criteria.
- Track attrition and nonresponse.
- Avoid overstating representativeness when sampling is limited or biased.
- In qualitative research, justify the sample by relevance, diversity, depth, and saturation logic where appropriate.
- In quantitative work, justify the sample by power, precision, or model requirements.

## Measurement and Instruments
- Every important construct needs an operational definition.
- Use validated instruments when possible, especially for established constructs.
- Adaptations to instruments should be documented and justified.
- Reliability should be assessed where relevant.
- Measurement error should be considered in interpretation.
- For surveys, wording, order effects, response scales, and missingness matter.
- For observational coding, codebooks, training, and inter-rater agreement are important.
- For digital or trace data, proxies should not be mistaken for the construct itself without validation.

## Data Management
- Data collection protocols should be documented before execution where possible.
- File naming, versioning, storage, and access rules reduce error and confusion.
- Metadata and codebooks are essential for reuse and interpretation.
- Sensitive data requires controlled access and secure storage.
- Cleaning decisions should be logged.
- Missing data handling should be justified rather than improvised.
- Data transformations, exclusions, merges, and derived variables should be reproducible.
- Preservation and archival plans matter for long-term transparency.

## Statistical and Analytical Practice
- Analysis should be linked directly to the research question and design.
- Assumptions should be checked and reported.
- Multiple testing and researcher degrees of freedom should be controlled or disclosed.
- Exploratory and confirmatory analyses should be distinguished clearly.
- Robustness checks strengthen confidence in findings.
- Outlier handling should be principled and transparent.
- Model selection should be justified rather than optimized only for fit.
- Predictive metrics and explanatory metrics serve different purposes.
- Visualizations should clarify rather than exaggerate patterns.
- Sensitivity analysis is valuable when assumptions are uncertain.

## Qualitative Analysis Practice
- Analysis should move systematically from raw material to interpretation.
- Coding frameworks should be documented.
- Theme development should be grounded in evidence, not just intuition.
- Reflexivity is part of quality: researchers should consider how their position influences interpretation.
- Thick description improves interpretive value.
- Quotations and examples should be representative and analytically meaningful.
- Negative cases and disconfirming evidence should be examined.
- Analytic memos and audit trails improve transparency.

## Literature Review
- A literature review should precede substantial new research unless the project is explicitly exploratory and time-constrained.
- Reviews should map what is known, disputed, under-theorized, and methodologically weak.
- Search strategies should be documented when rigor matters.
- Conceptual definitions must be consistent across sources or differences must be discussed.
- Strong reviews compare evidence quality, not just findings.
- Reviews should identify gaps carefully; “few studies exist” is not itself a gap unless the missing knowledge matters.
- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses generally provide stronger synthesis than isolated studies.

## Common Risks
- Confirmation bias: seeking or privileging evidence that supports an expected conclusion.
- Publication bias: positive or novel findings being reported more often than null results.
- P-hacking: trying many analyses until one appears significant.
- HARKing: presenting hypotheses developed after seeing the data as if they were specified beforehand.
- Scope creep: “interesting tangents” diluting the core question and design.
- Measurement mismatch: using poor proxies for key constructs.
- Sampling bias: drawing conclusions from a non-representative or distorted sample.
- Overgeneralization: extending findings beyond the population, setting, or intervention studied.
- Confounding: attributing effects to the wrong cause.
- Data leakage: contaminating analysis or predictive modeling with information unavailable at decision time.
- Omitted methods detail: making the work difficult to assess or replicate.
- Selective reporting: only highlighting favorable findings or subgroups.
- Underpowered studies: drawing strong conclusions from insufficient evidence.
- Misuse of significance: treating p-values as proof of truth or importance.
- Tool overreach: sophisticated methods being used without methodological justification.
- Ethical blind spots: insufficient attention to consent, privacy, harm, or power relations.

## Human Subjects and Ethics
- Human participants research may require ethics review before data collection.
- Informed consent should be meaningful, not merely procedural.
- Risks, burdens, benefits, and confidentiality should be considered explicitly.
- Vulnerable populations require additional safeguards.
- Privacy and data protection obligations apply throughout the lifecycle.
- Deception, if used, requires strong justification and appropriate debriefing.
- Researchers should consider downstream harms from interpretation, labeling, or intervention.
- Community impact matters, not just individual participant risk.
- Ethical research includes responsible dissemination and avoidance of misleading communication.

## Reproducibility and Open Science
- Pre-registration strengthens confirmatory work by reducing undisclosed flexibility.
- Registered reports can improve design quality by separating study approval from outcome favorability.
- Open materials, open data, and open code increase scrutiny and reuse when ethically feasible.
- Reproducible workflows reduce hidden analytical error.
- Version-controlled analysis scripts are preferable to undocumented manual processing.
- Transparency about what cannot be shared is better than silent omission.
- Replication is part of scientific progress, not a sign that prior work failed.

## Best Practices
- Conduct a literature review before launching major new research.
- Distinguish exploratory research from confirmatory research.
- Pre-register hypotheses and core analysis plans for confirmatory work.
- Write a research plan before data collection begins.
- Align research question, design, measures, and analysis from the outset.
- Use multiple reviewers for findings that will drive significant decisions.
- Pilot instruments or procedures when feasible.
- Document assumptions, deviations, and unexpected events.
- Build in quality checks during collection, not only at the end.
- Use triangulation where a single method is likely to be insufficient.
- Report null, mixed, and contradictory findings.
- Keep a decision log for major methodological choices.
- Invite methodological critique before finalizing major claims.
- Treat replication, robustness, and sensitivity analysis as core quality work.
- Define stakeholder expectations early so the research is not judged by the wrong criteria.

## Workflow Stages
- Problem framing: define the topic, need, decision context, and intended contribution.
- Question formulation: specify research questions or hypotheses.
- Literature review: establish prior art, theory, and gap.
- Design selection: choose method, sample, measures, instruments, and analysis approach.
- Ethics and approvals: obtain required review and permissions.
- Pre-registration or protocol finalization: lock key decisions where appropriate.
- Data collection: execute according to plan while logging deviations.
- Data preparation: clean, code, merge, transform, and document.
- Analysis: perform descriptive, inferential, interpretive, or modeling steps aligned to the design.
- Interpretation: relate findings back to the question, literature, and limitations.
- Reporting: communicate methods, findings, uncertainty, and implications.
- Dissemination and maintenance: publish, archive, present, and correct if needed.

## Reporting Standards
- Methods should be complete enough for informed critique and potential replication.
- Results should separate description from interpretation.
- Tables and figures should support the argument rather than decorate it.
- Limitations should be explicit and substantive.
- Contradictory, weak, or null findings should be included when relevant.
- Statistical reporting should include effect sizes and uncertainty where applicable.
- Qualitative reporting should include sufficient evidence for interpretive claims.
- Language should reflect uncertainty and scope honestly.
- Conclusions should state what the study contributes and what remains unresolved.

## Decision-Making Use of Research
- Research should inform decisions, not substitute for judgment.
- High-stakes decisions should not rely on a single weak study.
- Evidence should be weighed by quality, relevance, and convergence.
- Decision contexts often require combining research evidence with operational, ethical, and stakeholder considerations.
- When evidence is uncertain, recommendations should reflect that uncertainty.
- Action thresholds should be explicit when research informs policy or intervention.

## Common Heuristics
- What exactly is the claim?
- What evidence supports it?
- What design produced that evidence?
- What alternative explanations remain plausible?
- Does the method match the question?
- How strong is the uncertainty?
- What population and context does this actually apply to?
- What would change your mind about the conclusion?
- What is missing from the reported account?
- Could the same data support a weaker, more accurate claim?

## Prior Art
- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses over isolated single studies.
- Mixed methods for questions that require both measurement and interpretation.
- Triangulation through multiple independent methods or data sources.
- Pre-registration and registered reports for confirmatory work.
- Research ethics review for studies involving human participants.
- Replication studies and robustness analysis as part of cumulative science.
- Case study, ethnographic, survey, experimental, quasi-experimental, and longitudinal traditions.
- Open science practices for transparency and reproducibility.

## Domain Vocabulary
- Research question: the specific question the study is designed to answer.
- Hypothesis: a testable expectation about a relationship, effect, or pattern.
- Operationalization: translating a construct into a measurable form.
- Variable: a measured or manipulated attribute in the study.
- Construct: an abstract concept such as trust, engagement, or motivation.
- Validity: the extent to which inferences or measurements are justified.
- Reliability: the consistency of a measure or coding process.
- Confounder: a factor that may distort the apparent relationship between variables.
- Sampling frame: the accessible set from which a sample is drawn.
- Power: the study’s ability to detect an effect if one exists.
- Effect size: the magnitude of a relationship or difference.
- Triangulation: using multiple methods, sources, or lenses to strengthen inference.
- Pre-registration: publicly documenting hypotheses and plans before seeing outcomes.
- Replication: repeating a study to assess whether findings hold.
- Reproducibility: obtaining the same results from the same data and procedures.
- Reflexivity: critical attention to the researcher’s influence on the research process.
- Audit trail: documented record of decisions made during the project.
- Null result: a finding that does not support the expected effect or association.
